### Primary Source: Autogenic Training: A Meta-Analysis of Clinical Outcome Studies
- **Authors**: Friedhelm Stetter, Sirko Kupper
- **Year**: 2002
- **Title**: Autogenic Training: A Meta-Analysis of Clinical Outcome Studies
- **Journal**: Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback, Vol. 27(1), 45-98
- **URL**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1014576505223
- **Key Findings**: Meta-analysis of 73 controlled outcome studies (published 1952-1999), with 60 studies (35 RCTs) qualifying for inclusion. Medium-to-large effect sizes occurred for pre-post comparisons of disease-specific AT effects, with RCTs showing larger effect sizes. When AT was compared to real control conditions, medium effect sizes were found. Positive effects demonstrated for tension headache/migraine, mild-to-moderate essential hypertension, coronary heart disease, asthma, somatoform pain disorder, Raynaud's disease, anxiety disorders, mild-to-moderate depression/dysthymia, and functional sleep disorders. Unspecific AT effects on mood, cognitive performance, quality of life, and physiological variables tended to be even larger than main effects.
- **Effect Size**: d = 0.68 (biological indices), d = 0.75 (psychological indices) versus control conditions

## Implementation Protocol

### Clinical Guidelines
Autogenic training is a structured self-relaxation technique developed by German psychiatrist Johannes Heinrich Schultz in the 1930s, with the definitive six-volume text published with Wolfgang Luthe (Schultz & Luthe, 1969). The method consists of six standard exercises that use auto-suggestions of heaviness, warmth, cardiac regulation, respiratory regulation, abdominal warmth, and forehead cooling to elicit a psychophysiological relaxation response.

AT is classified as a self-hypnotic technique because it relies on passive concentration and autosuggestion rather than external hypnotic induction. The practitioner teaches the method, but the client ultimately practices independently. Traditional AT training requires 8-10 weeks of daily practice to master all six exercises, though abbreviated protocols (3-4 exercises) are commonly used in performance and clinical settings.

The technique should be practiced in a quiet environment, in a comfortable position (seated or reclined), with eyes closed. Each exercise is practiced for 1-2 minutes before adding the next in sequence. The full sequence takes 5-8 minutes once learned.

### Step-by-Step Process

**Phase 1: Preparation and Settling (30-60 seconds)**
1. Assume a comfortable position (reclined, seated with arms supported, or "coachman" posture)
2. Close eyes gently
3. Take 2-3 slow, natural breaths
4. Mentally repeat: "I am at peace" or "I am completely calm" (background formula)
5. Release any intentional effort; adopt an attitude of passive concentration

**Phase 2: Heaviness Exercise (1 minute)**
1. Focus attention on the dominant arm
2. Mentally repeat 3-6 times: "My right arm is heavy" (or left arm for left-dominant)
3. Allow the sensation of heaviness to develop naturally without forcing
4. Extend to both arms: "Both arms are heavy"
5. Extend to legs: "My legs are heavy"
6. Full body: "My arms and legs are heavy"
7. Return to background formula: "I am at peace"

**Phase 3: Warmth Exercise (1 minute)**
1. Focus attention on the dominant arm
2. Mentally repeat 3-6 times: "My right arm is warm"
3. Allow the sensation of warmth to develop naturally through passive concentration
4. Extend to both arms: "Both arms are warm"
5. Extend to legs: "My legs are warm"
6. Full body: "My arms and legs are warm and heavy"
7. Return to background formula: "I am at peace"

**Phase 4: Cardiac Regulation (30-45 seconds)**
1. Place awareness on the heartbeat (optionally place hand on chest initially)
2. Mentally repeat 3-6 times: "My heartbeat is calm and regular"
3. Do not attempt to change heart rate; simply observe and suggest calm regularity
4. Return to background formula: "I am at peace"

**Phase 5: Respiratory Regulation (30-45 seconds)**
1. Allow breathing to find its own natural rhythm
2. Mentally repeat 3-6 times: "My breathing is calm and regular" or "It breathes me"
3. The phrase "It breathes me" emphasizes passive surrender rather than active control
4. Return to background formula: "I am at peace"

**Phase 6: Abdominal Warmth (30-45 seconds)**
1. Focus attention on the solar plexus / upper abdominal region
2. Mentally repeat 3-6 times: "My solar plexus is warm"
3. Visualize gentle warmth radiating from the center of the body
4. Return to background formula: "I am at peace"

**Phase 7: Forehead Cooling and Cancellation (30-60 seconds)**
1. Focus attention on the forehead
2. Mentally repeat 3-6 times: "My forehead is pleasantly cool"
3. This exercise creates alert clarity while maintaining physical relaxation
4. Cancellation procedure: Flex arms, take a deep breath, open eyes
5. The cancellation is essential to prevent lingering drowsiness and restore full alertness

### Contraindications
- **Severe cardiovascular conditions**: The cardiac regulation exercise should be omitted or modified for clients with serious heart conditions without medical clearance
- **Acute psychosis or severe psychiatric disorders**: AT may intensify internal focus in destabilizing ways
- **Insulin-dependent diabetes**: AT can affect blood glucose levels; medical coordination required
- **Hypotension**: AT may further lower blood pressure in already hypotensive individuals
- **Children under 5**: The method requires cognitive capacity for self-directed attention and internal body awareness

### Cautions
- Clients with a strong need for control may initially struggle with passive concentration; reassure that effort is counterproductive
- Some individuals may experience autogenic discharges (spontaneous emotional releases, muscle twitches, temperature sensations) during early practice; these are normal and self-limiting
- The cardiac exercise may provoke anxiety in individuals with health anxiety or cardiac phobia; can be modified or skipped initially
- "My forehead is cool" should NOT be changed to "My forehead is cold" as cold sensations can trigger tension headaches
- Abbreviated protocols (heaviness + warmth only) are suitable for performance contexts where full medical screening is not available
- Traditional AT requires weeks of progressive training; shortened guided versions are effective but less potent than fully trained self-practice

## Practitioner Scripts & Language Patterns

### Example Script Excerpt
"Make yourself comfortable... close your eyes gently... and take a few easy breaths... There is nothing you need to do right now... nothing you need to figure out... Simply allow yourself to be here...

Now bring your attention to your right arm... and silently repeat after me... My right arm is heavy... my right arm is heavy... my right arm is heavy... Just notice whatever you notice... there's no need to make anything happen... simply repeat the words and let your body respond in its own time... My right arm is heavy... my right arm is heavy... my right arm is heavy...

Good... I am at peace...

And now... My right arm is warm... my right arm is warm... my right arm is warm... Perhaps you notice a gentle warmth beginning to spread... or perhaps it comes later... it doesn't matter... simply continue with passive attention... My right arm is warm... my right arm is warm...

Good... I am at peace...

Now gently aware of your heartbeat... My heartbeat is calm and regular... my heartbeat is calm and regular... just noticing... not changing... simply aware... my heartbeat is calm and regular...

I am at peace...

And now your breathing... It breathes me... it breathes me... it breathes me... Letting go of any control over the breath... allowing the body to breathe itself... it breathes me...

I am at peace...

My solar plexus is warm... warm energy at the center of my body... my solar plexus is warm...

I am at peace...

And finally... my forehead is pleasantly cool... my forehead is pleasantly cool... alert and clear... my forehead is pleasantly cool...

I am at peace... completely calm... completely at peace...

And now, to return to full alertness... flex your arms... take a deep breath... and open your eyes..."

### Specific Language Patterns

1. **Background formula repetition**: "I am at peace... I am completely calm..." (creates the container for all subsequent exercises)
2. **Passive voice autosuggestion**: "My right arm IS heavy..." (states a condition rather than commanding)
3. **"It breathes me" formulation**: Removes agency from the client, promoting passive surrender to autonomic processes
4. **Progressive body extension**: "My arm... both arms... my legs... arms and legs..." (gradual expansion of awareness)
5. **Non-striving permission**: "There is nothing you need to do... nothing to figure out..." (prevents effortful trying)
6. **Sensation permission**: "Perhaps you notice... or perhaps it comes later..." (removes performance pressure)
7. **Repetition cadence**: Each formula repeated 3-6 times in steady rhythm (creates hypnotic entrainment)
8. **Cancellation command**: "Flex arms... deep breath... open eyes" (crisp, directive tone for full alertness return)
9. **Transition bridges**: "Good... I am at peace..." (between each exercise, resets and deepens)
10. **Temperature gradient language**: Heavy -> Warm -> Calm -> Peaceful -> Cool (follows the natural sequence from peripheral relaxation to mental clarity)

### Milton Model Elements
- **Truisms**: "The body knows how to relax on its own..." (undeniable physiological truth)
- **Presuppositions**: "As the warmth continues to spread..." (presupposes warmth is already present)
- **Embedded commands**: "You can FEEL HEAVY and COMFORTABLE now..."
- **Nominalization**: "This heaviness... this warmth... this calm..." (makes fleeting sensations seem substantial)
- **Temporal predicates**: "Beginning to notice... continuing to deepen... allowing this to develop..."
- **Pacing and leading**: "You're sitting comfortably [pace], your eyes are closed [pace], and warmth is beginning to spread [lead]..."
- **Analog marking**: Vocal emphasis and slower pacing on key sensation words: "HEAVY... WARM... CALM..."
- **Binds of comparable alternatives**: "The heaviness might come quickly, or it might develop gradually..." (both outcomes are success)

## Delivery Notes
- **Tone**: Calm, steady, monotonous (deliberately so). The voice itself should model the state being induced
- **Pacing**: Very slow and rhythmic. Each formula repetition should take 5-8 seconds. Pauses of 3-5 seconds between repetitions
- **Volume**: Soft, consistent. Avoid sudden volume changes that could startle
- **Breathing**: Practitioner should breathe audibly and slowly, providing a pacing stimulus for the client
- **Duration**: Guided session: 5-8 minutes (abbreviated 3-exercise); 8-12 minutes (full 6-exercise). Self-practice: 5-15 minutes
- **Repetition**: Each formula repeated 3-6 times per exercise. Consistency of wording is important
- **Cancellation**: The cancellation procedure should be delivered in a noticeably firmer, brisker tone to facilitate return to alertness
- **Music**: Not traditionally used in AT. Silence or very minimal ambient sound is preferred to maintain internal focus

## Evidence Level Assessment
- **Rating**: Strong
- **Rationale**: Autogenic training has one of the most robust evidence bases of any relaxation/self-hypnotic technique, supported by decades of European and international research.

**Strengths:**
1. Major meta-analysis (Stetter & Kupper, 2002) covering 60 studies with 35 RCTs demonstrating medium-to-large effect sizes
2. Consistent replication across anxiety, depression, hypertension, pain, insomnia, and cardiovascular conditions
3. Multiple systematic reviews (Kanji & Ernst, 2000; Manzoni et al., 2008; Kim & Kim, 2019) all confirming efficacy
4. Nearly 100 years of clinical use since Schultz's original development (1932)
5. Standardized protocol allows consistent delivery and research replication
6. Self-administered nature supports long-term maintenance and cost-effectiveness
7. Physiological mechanisms well-documented (autonomic nervous system regulation, cortisol reduction, blood pressure effects)
8. Extensive use in European sports psychology with documented performance benefits

**Limitations:**
1. Some older studies have methodological weaknesses (pre-RCT era designs)
2. Requires consistent daily practice (8-10 weeks) for full mastery, which limits adherence
3. Passive concentration is counterintuitive for some individuals, particularly those with high control needs
4. Less studied for severe psychiatric conditions (psychotic disorders, bipolar disorder)
5. Comparisons with other relaxation techniques generally show equivalent rather than superior effects

**Conclusion**: Autogenic training is a well-established, evidence-based self-regulation technique with strong meta-analytic support. Its standardized protocol, extensive research history, self-administered nature, and broad clinical applicability make it one of the most robustly supported relaxation techniques available. Particularly well-suited for structured, disciplined individuals who benefit from systematic approaches.

## Additional Notes

### Historical Context
- **1920s**: Johannes Heinrich Schultz, a German psychiatrist and neurologist, began developing autogenic training based on his observations of patients undergoing hypnosis with Oskar Vogt
- **1932**: Schultz published "Das Autogene Training" (Autogenic Training), the first comprehensive text on the method
- **1950s-1960s**: Wolfgang Luthe, a student of Schultz, expanded the method and published the six-volume "Autogenic Therapy" (1969) in English, making AT accessible to the international community
- **1970s-present**: AT became widely adopted in European sports psychology, occupational health, and clinical medicine. It remains more commonly used in Europe than in North America, where progressive muscle relaxation (Jacobson) tends to dominate
- **Contemporary**: Modified and abbreviated AT protocols are used in performance psychology, mindfulness-based programs, and integrative medicine

### The Six Standard Exercises (Schultz & Luthe, 1969)
1. **Heaviness** (Schwere): Muscular relaxation through suggestion of limb heaviness
2. **Warmth** (Warme): Vascular dilation through suggestion of limb warmth
3. **Cardiac regulation** (Herz): Heart rate calming through awareness and suggestion
4. **Respiratory regulation** (Atem): Breathing normalization through passive observation
5. **Abdominal warmth** (Sonnengeflecht): Solar plexus warmth activating digestive parasympathetic tone
6. **Forehead cooling** (Stirn): Mental clarity through suggestion of cool forehead

### Physiological Mechanisms
- **Peripheral vasodilation**: Warmth suggestions measurably increase skin temperature in extremities (increased blood flow)
- **Muscle relaxation**: Heaviness suggestions reduce EMG activity comparable to progressive muscle relaxation
- **Autonomic shift**: The full sequence promotes parasympathetic dominance, reducing sympathetic arousal
- **Cortisol reduction**: Studies document decreased salivary cortisol following AT practice
- **Heart rate variability**: AT practice is associated with increased HRV, indicating improved vagal tone
- **EEG changes**: AT produces alpha and theta wave increases similar to meditation and light hypnosis

### Abbreviated Protocols for Performance Contexts
For performance settings where the full 6-exercise protocol is impractical:
- **3-Exercise Quick Version** (3-4 minutes): Heaviness + Warmth + "I am at peace" (background formula only)
- **Sport-Specific Version** (4-5 minutes): Heaviness + Warmth + Breathing + Cancellation (omits cardiac and abdominal for simplicity)
- **Pre-Sleep Version** (5-7 minutes): Full sequence minus forehead cooling and cancellation (allows drift into sleep)
